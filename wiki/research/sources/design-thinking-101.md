## Summary

This source is Sarah Gibbons' article on the fundamentals of Design Thinking, published by the Nielsen Norman Group. It defines Design Thinking as both a user-centric problem-solving ideology and a hands-on process consisting of six distinct phases (Empathize, Define, Ideate, Prototype, Test, Implement), structured under three primary buckets: Understand, Explore, and Materialize. It emphasizes execution ("design doing") and adaptability to achieve competitive differentiation and true innovation.

## Extracted Content

- **Dual Definition**:
  - **Ideology**: A hands-on, user-centric approach to problem-solving leads to innovation, creating competitive differentiation.
  - **Process**: The structured 6-phase framework.
- **The Six Phases**:
  1. *Empathize* (Understand): Gather observations to understand user behaviors, feelings, and frustrations.
  2. *Define* (Understand): Analyze observations to isolate pain points and unmet user needs.
  3. *Ideate* (Explore): Brainstorm high volumes of creative, unconstrained solutions.
  4. *Prototype* (Explore): Create low-fidelity, tactile representations of concepts to test feasibility and impact.
  5. *Test* (Materialize): Gather feedback from real users on the prototypes.
  6. *Implement* (Materialize): Execute the vision, turning design thinking into "design doing" to deliver real-world value.
- **Advantages**: Focuses resources on solving the correct problems, leverages collective team expertise, establishes a shared vocabulary, and fosters systematic innovation.
- **Iterative Nature**: The stages are cyclical scaffolding, not a linear sequence. Teams must loop back to research or redefine problems as prototypes uncover new realities.
