## Sintesi

Designing for Artificial Intelligence represents a paradigm shift from building transactional interfaces (typing text and hitting "submit") to designing human-machine relationships. According to IBM's framework, AI systems are trained rather than programmed, meaning they understand, reason, learn, and interact dynamically. To build effective AI, designers must focus on three core lenses—**Purpose**, **Value**, and **Trust**—cultivating a symbiotic partnership that augments human decision-making.

---

## The Three AI Design Lenses

All design decisions in an AI system must be evaluated through these core perspectives:

1. **Purpose**
   - The reason the user engages with the system.
   - It is not static; it evolves as the user and the system grow together through cooperative interactions.
2. **Value**
   - The augmented capabilities the system provides to improve the user's life.
   - AI must deliver actionable **insights**, not just raw data (insights are the destination, data is the fuel).
3. **Trust**
   - The user's willingness to build a bond and rely on the system.
   - Predicated on data security, the **feeling of human control**, and the accuracy/reliability of the output.

---

## Four Qualities of AI

Traditional computing executes pre-programmed instructions. AI simulates human thought processes through four qualities:

- **Understands:** Ingests structured and unstructured data (text, sensory) in context, at scale and speed.
- **Reasons:** Evaluates data toward specific goals, forms hypotheses, and presents prioritized recommendations to aid human decision-making.
- **Learns:** Continuously improves through experience. AI is **trained by experts, not programmed**, accumulating insight from every interaction.
- **Interacts:** Flows unobtrusively into user workflows, communicating naturally to sustain a long-term relationship.

## Adaptation of Knapp's Relational Development Model

To understand how a human forms a bond with an AI ("coming together"), IBM adapts Knapp's psychological model of relationship development:

```
  ┌──────────────┐     ┌──────────────┐     ┌──────────────┐     ┌──────────────┐     ┌──────────┐
  │  Initiating  │───> │ Experimenting│───> │ Intensifying │───> │ Integrating  │───> │ Bonding  │
  └──────────────┘     └──────────────┘     └──────────────┘     └──────────────┘     └──────────┘
  Snap judgments,      Finding common       Reciprocal           AI fits              Bond through
  first impressions.   value & interest.    sharing & trust.     unobtrusively.       trust/appreciation.
```

- **Initiating:** The user forms initial impressions. If positive, they proceed.
- **Experimenting:** The user explores the AI's boundaries, testing capabilities and finding immediate value.
- **Intensifying:** The user begins sharing more specific inputs; the AI adapts, showing deeper personalization.
- **Integrating:** The AI integrates seamlessly into the user's daily habits and tools.
- **Bonding:** A permanent partnership based on trust, where the system is seen as an essential assistant.

## The AI/Human Context Model

Human-AI interactions follow a cycle that updates the machine's knowledge base continuously:

1. **Intent:** The goals, wants, and needs of users and businesses. Sets the purpose.
2. **Data & Policy:** The raw data gathered, governed by security and compliance policies.
3. **Understanding:** The process of contextualizing data within the domain (Machine Learning).
4. **Reasoning:** The application of logic to determine the best actions or outputs.
5. **Knowledge:** The summation of past data, insights, and learned attributes.
6. **Expression:** How the AI speaks or acts based on the user's preferences.
7. **User Reaction:** The user's direct or indirect response to the AI's output.
8. **Learning:** Feedback loops where the user trains the system to correct or improve.
9. **Outcome:** The real-world consequence of using the AI to solve the user's problem.
