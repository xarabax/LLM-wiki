## Summary

Jakob Nielsen's 10 general principles for interaction design, originally developed in 1990 in collaboration with Rolf Molich and refined in 1994. They are broad rules of thumb ("heuristics") rather than specific usability guidelines, aimed at minimizing user cognitive load and ensuring visibility, control, consistency, error prevention, and efficient recovery.

## Extracted Content

The document presents the core principles of usability heuristics:
1. **Visibility of System Status** — Keep users informed about what is going on.
2. **Match Between System and Real World** — Speak the users' language and follow real-world conventions.
3. **User Control and Freedom** — Support undo, redo, and clear emergency exits.
4. **Consistency and Standards** — Follow platform and industry conventions (Jakob's Law).
5. **Error Prevention** — Eliminate error-prone conditions or request confirmation before committing.
6. **Recognition Rather than Recall** — Minimize memory load by making elements and options visible.
7. **Flexibility and Efficiency of Use** — Provide accelerators (shortcuts, gestures) for expert users and customization.
8. **Aesthetic and Minimalist Design** — Focus UI design and content on the essentials.
9. **Help Users Recognize, Diagnose, and Recover from Errors** — Express error messages in plain language, indicate the problem, and suggest solutions.
10. **Help and Documentation** — Provide searchable, contextual, and task-focused documentation.

### Historical Context
Developed originally in 1990, refined via factor analysis of 249 usability problems in 1994. The 10 heuristics have remained unchanged for over 26 years, proving their long-term relevance across multiple generations of user interfaces.
